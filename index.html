What is an Operational Amplifier?

An operational amplifier is a high-gain device with differential inputs. Its basic action without feedback is:

\[ V_{\text{out}} = A (V_+ - V_-) \]

  • V+: non-inverting input
  • V-: inverting input
  • A: very high open-loop gain

By closing the loop (negative feedback), you gain control over the amplification, stability, and behavior of the circuit.

Active Filters Using Op‑Amps

Low-Pass Filter (LPF)

Cutoff Frequency:

\( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} \)

Transfer Function (1st-order):

\( H(s) = \frac{1}{1 + sRC} \)

Where s represents j\omega\)

Second-order form (with Q):

\( H(s) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{s^2 + \frac{\omega_0}{Q}s + \omega_0^2} \)

Important Notes: LPFs pass low frequencies and attenuate high ones. Q determines how sharp the roll-off is in second-order designs.

High-Pass Filter (HPF)

Cutoff Frequency:

\( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} \)

Transfer Function (1st-order):

\( H(s) = \frac{sRC}{1 + sRC} \)

Second-order form (with Q):

\( H(s) = \frac{s^2}{s^2 + \frac{\omega_0}{Q}s + \omega_0^2} \)

Important Notes: HPFs block low frequencies, allow high. High Q gives a sharp transition but may cause overshoot.

Band-Pass Filter (BPF)

Center Frequency:

\( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{R_1 R_2 C_1 C_2}} \)

Transfer Function:

\( H(s) = \frac{\frac{\omega_0}{Q}s}{s^2 + \frac{\omega_0}{Q}s + \omega_0^2} \)

Q Factor:

\( Q = \frac{f_0}{f_2 - f_1} \)

Important Notes: The Q factor determines the sharpness of the passband. High Q = narrow band. Useful in RF, bio-signal processing.

Band-Stop Filter (Notch / BSF)

Center Frequency:

\( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{R_1 R_2 C_1 C_2}} \)

Transfer Function:

\( H(s) = \frac{s^2 + \omega_0^2}{s^2 + \frac{\omega_0}{Q}s + \omega_0^2} \)

Q Factor:

\( Q = \frac{f_0}{f_2 - f_1} \)

Important Notes: Deep, narrow notch = high Q. Used to eliminate hums or specific frequencies. Inverse of BPF.

Power Supply Considerations

Op-amps can operate from dual supplies (e.g., ±12 V) or single supplies (+5 V or +3.3 V). In single-supply designs, a virtual ground is created at mid-rail. Rail-to-rail op-amps can output signals near their supply rails, expanding usable range in low-voltage circuits.

Noise & Distortion

Op‑amps introduce thermal (Johnson–Nyquist) and flicker (1/f) noise. Total harmonic distortion (THD) quantifies waveform purity. Low-noise and precision parts, good PCB layout, and proper bypassing are essential for clean analog signals.

Thermal Effects

Op-amp parameters like input offset voltage and bias currents drift with temperature. In high-precision circuits, compensation schemes such as using matched parts or adding temperature feedback help maintain accuracy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is negative feedback essential?

It stabilizes gain, reduces distortion, sets bandwidth, and keeps inputs at a virtual short.

What determines signal bandwidth?

The op-amp's gain‑bandwidth product (GBW) and the closed-loop gain define the usable frequency range.

Single vs. dual-supply?

Dual rails give symmetric swings. Single-supply uses virtual ground; rail-to-rail devices extend input/output ranges.

How do we minimize noise and distortion?

Select low-noise parts, maintain proper layout, and use power-supply decoupling.

Why is thermal drift important?

Temperature shifts offset and bias. Precision circuits often include compensation or calibration.
